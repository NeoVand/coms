# Part XI, Chapter — AS 7007 1997 — The Florida Router That Ate the Internet

## The hook
Most-specific wins, by definition. AS 7007 did not hijack anything on purpose — it simply announced everything as a /24, and the entire internet routed through a single underpowered Florida router. On 25 April 1997, that router took most of the public internet offline for over an hour, and taught the BGP community a lesson it spent the next twenty-five years acting on.

## The story

### The Florida Router That Ate the Internet
On 25 April 1997, an MAI Network Services router in Florida — operating as autonomous system 7007 — received the global BGP routing table from its upstream. About fifty thousand prefixes at the time. Then a misconfiguration in a packet inspection appliance redistributed every entry back into BGP as a /24, originating from AS 7007 itself.

How BGP actually announces and withdraws routes — OPEN, UPDATE, KEEPALIVE messages every thirty seconds over TCP port 179, the path-vector model, eBGP versus iBGP — is the BGP episode. For now, hold onto one rule: BGP's tie-breaker is "most-specific prefix wins." A /24 always beats a /16 or a /8.

So when AS 7007 announced "I am the next hop for 8.8.8.0/24, and 8.8.10.0/24, and 8.8.11.0/24, and tens of thousands more," every BGP router on the planet preferred those new, more-specific routes over the legitimate aggregated announcements from the actual prefix owners. From the global routing table's point of view, an underpowered router in Florida was now the apparent origin of much of the internet's address space.

### The Cascade
Within minutes, the rest of the internet was sending the entire internet's traffic through that single Florida router. The device had nowhere near the forwarding capacity of a tier-1 backbone. It collapsed. Sprint, MAI's upstream, had no filters and propagated the routes to its peers, who propagated them onwards. Sprint's network could not handle the redirected traffic either. Routers crashed. Sprint went largely dark.

What made the recovery so painful was that there was no central kill switch. There was no way to tell BGP "drop those announcements" globally. Every upstream provider had to manually install filters rejecting the bogus /24s from AS 7007. Sprint took the lead on the cleanup — pulling cable, updating filter lists, waiting for BGP convergence. Even after MAI withdrew the routes, propagation took hours to drain because the bad routes had been cached widely across the internet. Most of the global internet was unreachable for over an hour. Aftershocks lasted for days.

The first detailed public communication about what had just happened was Vince Bono's apology email to the NANOG mailing list that same day, titled "7007 Explanation and Apology." It is preserved in the NANOG archives. It is a remarkably calm and detailed admission that became a model for incident communication in the operator community.

### Three controls that did not exist in 1997
Three things every modern transit provider does as a matter of course did not exist as standard practice in 1997.

Max-prefix limits — automatically drop a BGP session that announces an unreasonable number of routes. If a customer normally announces ten prefixes and suddenly tries to announce fifty thousand, the session goes down before the damage spreads.

Prefix filters — only accept announcements for the specific prefixes a customer is documented to own. Sprint accepting MAI's /24 fragments of the entire internet was possible only because Sprint had no such filter in place.

RPKI and Route Origin Validation — cryptographically validate the origin AS before accepting a route. The prefix-holder publishes a Route Origin Authorisation, the receiving router enforces the check, and any mismatch is rejected.

None of these were standard in 1997. All three exist now in large part because of incidents like AS 7007. RPKI is the centrepiece of that long deployment story, and it is the second half of this chapter.

### Why It Took Twenty-Five Years to Fix
RPKI was specified in 2008. It took until 2026 to cross fifty per cent of advertised IP space covered. The reason is the structure of BGP deployment.

For RPKI to fix the AS 7007 problem, two parties have to participate. The prefix-holder must publish a Route Origin Authorisation — a signed statement saying "AS X is allowed to originate prefix Y." And the receiving router must enforce Route Origin Validation, the runtime check that compares each BGP UPDATE against those authorisations. The result is Valid, Invalid, or NotFound. Valid and NotFound are accepted. Invalid is dropped.

For the first decade after specification, only a handful of large networks ran the validation step. Smaller operators pointed out — correctly — that running validation when most prefixes were not yet signed meant dropping legitimate routes from peers who simply had not signed yet. So nobody enforced. Without enforcement, signing your prefixes had no upside. Classic chicken-and-egg.

What broke the deadlock was a series of high-profile incidents. The 2018 Amazon Route 53 hijack that drained MyEtherWallet wallets. The 2018 Iranian Telegram hijack. The 2019 SafeHost incident where China Telecom leaked twenty thousand European prefixes. Each one made unsigned networks look negligent. By 2022, the major hyperscalers and tier-1 transit providers were enforcing. By 2026, over half of advertised IP space was covered by ROAs, and the deployment crossed the inflection point at which most internet traffic is bound for an RPKI-secured destination. ASPA — the AS-path validation extension that goes beyond origin validation to detect path hijacks and route leaks — is the next chapter of the story.

## The figures

### AS 7007 — The Day a Florida ISP Took Down the Internet
A small Florida ISP's misconfigured Bay Networks router de-aggregated the entire global routing table into /24s and re-originated them all as itself. Multi-hour partial outage of the public internet on 25 April 1997, with Sprint customers especially affected, and aftershocks for days. The first time the BGP community truly understood that a single misconfigured router anywhere on earth could take down the entire internet.

### RPKI ROV Crosses 50% of IPv4 Prefixes
By May 2024, more than half of IPv4 routes had Route Origin Authorisations, and roughly three-quarters of IP traffic was bound for RPKI-secured destinations. Cloudflare's separate measurement of enforcement — autonomous systems that actually drop invalid routes — puts the directly-protected user population at about 261 million, around 6.5 per cent. But because almost every tier-1 transit provider drops invalids, indirect validation suppresses invalid-route propagation by a factor of two to three. ASPA, the path-hijack defence that complements RPKI's origin defence, is in IETF SIDROPS last call as of April 2026.

## What it taught the industry
Three controls became standard transit-provider practice in the years after AS 7007.

Max-prefix limits went in first, because they did not require coordination — a single transit provider could protect itself by configuring a sensible ceiling on each BGP session. If a customer normally announces ten prefixes, a session that suddenly tries to announce fifty thousand gets dropped automatically.

Prefix filters became table stakes for customer onboarding. The discipline of "only accept what the customer is documented to own" turned the question "what prefixes does this AS actually originate?" into a contractual artefact, maintained in the IRR — internet routing registry — databases.

RPKI and ROV took the longest because they required coordination across the entire ecosystem. The prefix-holder has to publish, and the receiving router has to enforce. The deployment story is twenty-five years long, hinges on a small number of high-profile incidents that made unsigned networks look negligent, and is still in motion. The how-it-actually-works story for BGP — including how a router resolves a BGP next-hop through OSPF or IS-IS, and how UPDATEs and WITHDRAWALs propagate across the global routing table — belongs to the BGP episode.
