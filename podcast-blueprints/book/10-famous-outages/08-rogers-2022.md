# Part XI, Chapter — Rogers 2022 — A Country Disconnected

## The hook
*"When a single private company is the de facto national emergency-services backbone, regulatory infrastructure has to treat it as critical national infrastructure."* That was the CRTC, Canada's telecom regulator, writing in August 2022. The trigger was 8 July 2022, when Rogers Communications went down for fifteen hours and took 911, debit-card payments, and a large chunk of Canadian daily life with it. This is the story of the day a country found out how much of itself ran on one network.

## The story

### When the ISP Is the Country
On 8 July 2022 at 04:45 EDT, Rogers Communications — Canada's largest ISP and mobile carrier, with over eleven million subscribers — went down nationally. For fifteen hours, Rogers customers had no internet, no mobile signal, and no landline. Rogers also runs the largest VoIP-based home phone service in Canada through its Hi-Speed Internet bundles, so the home phones went silent too. Interac, the national debit-card network, runs over Rogers connectivity, so Interac e-Transfer stopped nationwide and most debit transactions failed. And in many regions, 911 service stopped working.

The trigger was a misconfiguration during a planned maintenance update to the Rogers IP and MPLS core. The mechanics of how IP packets get routed hop by hop is the IP episode — what matters here is that the bad change overwhelmed the routers that do the routing. A bad route policy caused control-plane CPU exhaustion across the core. The routers spent so much CPU recomputing routes in response to the policy that they had no cycles left to forward traffic. The country's largest network ran out of room to think.

### A Country-Wide Cascade
The downstream effects revealed how deeply a single carrier's connectivity had been woven into Canadian infrastructure.

Banking and payments went first. Interac e-Transfer stopped nationwide. Most debit transactions failed at the point of sale. Some ATMs went offline. Several major retailers had to close because they could not accept payment at all. For a day, large parts of Canadian retail were cash-only, and most Canadians do not carry cash.

Healthcare felt it next. Several hospital systems lost connectivity to Rogers-hosted services. Telehealth platforms running over Rogers VoIP failed.

Government services failed too. Service Canada's online portals were unreachable from Rogers networks. The Canada Border Services Agency reported delays at land crossings.

And then emergency services. 911 calls failed in many areas. Some provinces issued public advisories telling residents to use a neighbour's phone, switch to another network, or walk to a fire station to report an emergency in person.

Recovery required Rogers engineers to log in to individual core routers over out-of-band management — which, thankfully, was on a separate network — and roll back the change by hand. By 21:00 EDT, partial service was back. Full service did not return until 14 July. Six days of intermittent issues for some customers, all from one maintenance window.

### The deeper structural lesson
The callout in the chapter is blunt about it. When a single private company's outage takes down national emergency services, the company is no longer just a private business — it is critical national infrastructure. The CRTC's post-incident investigation forced Rogers to enter mandatory reciprocal-roaming agreements with Bell and Telus, so that a future single-carrier outage would not strand emergency calls on a dead network. Australia, the UK, and Germany used the Rogers incident as a reference point for similar regulatory action.

### The Regulatory Aftermath
On 22 March 2023, the CRTC issued a formal decision requiring all major Canadian carriers to do four things.

First, implement reciprocal roaming for emergency calls within six months — so a phone unable to reach its own carrier can still complete a 911 call through any competing carrier's tower.

Second, submit detailed network architecture and dependency reports annually, including every system that depends on the carrier's network for life-safety functions.

Third, maintain out-of-band management independent of the production network for all core routing infrastructure. The thing that let Rogers eventually recover became the thing every Canadian carrier had to keep.

Fourth, conduct annual outage simulations with the federal government, including coordinated emergency-services failover.

These requirements are unusual in their specificity for a private telecommunications regulator. They reflect the structural change the Rogers outage forced: the recognition that some private companies have grown into roles that historically belonged to public utilities, with the same operational obligations.

## The figures

### Rogers — A Country Disconnected
Rogers ran one of Canada's three national telecom networks — wireless, wireline, internet, and the Interac point-of-sale debit-card system that powers Canadian retail. The IP core used BGP for routing between autonomous systems and OSPF for routing inside Rogers' own AS 812. That separation is the standard one — BGP carries the internet's full table of nearly a million prefixes between networks, and OSPF carries a much smaller link-state map inside a single network. The mechanics of how each protocol does that belong to their own future episodes.

The maintenance change was a tweak to BGP route policy meant to apply to a small set of routes. A missing filter let the entire BGP table — nearly a million prefixes — redistribute into OSPF. OSPF was never designed to carry a million routes. Every core router started flooding link-state updates trying to compute shortest-path trees over routes it should never have known about. CPUs and memory saturated. Routing software started crashing. The control plane melted.

From the outside, the result was a country going dark in stages. Wireless calls dropped. Wireline internet went down. Interac terminals stopped working at every store running on Rogers — meaning a large fraction of Canadian retail couldn't take payment for the day. Twelve million-plus customers were without service for a full business day. Hospitals, 911 operations, point-of-sale systems, and government services were all hit. The CRTC's 2024 executive summary identified the missing route filters and noted that lab testing had been skipped after a risk-scoring algorithm down-graded the change from "High" to "Low" because earlier phases had succeeded. Resolution required engineers physically present at core sites, manually rolling back the offending policy. The lesson the CRTC drew, in plain language: a single AS failing should not disconnect a country.

## What it taught the industry
The Rogers outage shifted how telecom regulators around the world think about single-carrier dependency. The Canadian rules — reciprocal emergency roaming, mandatory out-of-band management, annual outage simulations with the federal government, dependency reporting — became a template. Australia, the UK, and Germany cited the incident as they reconsidered their own carrier-resilience regimes. The structural change is the one the chapter's callout names: a private telecom large enough to carry national 911 traffic is now treated, at least for resilience purposes, as critical national infrastructure with public-utility obligations.

Inside Canadian carriers, the operational changes were just as concrete. Maintenance change processes added explicit checks for route-redistribution scope — the exact failure mode that took Rogers down. Risk-scoring algorithms that down-grade changes after early phases succeed got a hard look, because the very thing that made the algorithm comfortable was the thing that hid the blast radius. And out-of-band management stopped being an optional best practice and became regulator-mandated baseline.
