# Part III, Chapter — IPv6

## The hook

In October 2024, APNIC's Geoff Huston ran a linear extrapolation of IPv6 adoption and projected the transition might not complete until late 2045. He warned that v4-and-v6 coexistence may not be a transition at all. It may be a steady state. And then, on 28 March 2026, Google's IPv6 dashboard recorded 50.1% for the first time in history. Twenty-eight years after RFC 2460. This is the chapter on the slowest successful protocol migration in the history of the internet.

## The story

### Why "v6"? Because v5 Was Already Taken

Version 5 was already claimed — by the experimental ST and ST-II Stream Protocol for real-time transport. That left the candidate proposals to fight over later numbers. SIPP took 6, and SIPP won. The selection happened at the so-called BigTen retreat near Chicago, on 19 and 20 May 1994, where the IPng Directorate met with the working group chairs. SIPP-128 — the 128-bit-address variant — was formally selected at the Toronto IETF on 25 July 1994. The lineage runs through Steve Deering and Robert Hinden, and there is a separate Steve Deering episode for the architect's story.

The spec itself shipped as RFC 1883 in 1995, was revised as RFC 2460 in 1998, and only became STD 86, RFC 8200, in July 2017. That is 19 years between the working spec and the Internet Standard, and 22 years from the original. The address field grew from 32 bits to 128 bits — enough that, as the running joke goes, every grain of sand on Earth could have its own subnet. The header was simplified. Fragmentation was pushed out of routers and onto the source host. Multicast and link-local autoconfiguration — SLAAC, specified in RFC 4862 — became core mechanisms instead of bolt-ons.

Then it took twenty-eight years to reach 50% adoption. The reason is not technical. It is the chicken-and-egg of network effects. How the protocol actually works on the wire — the 40-byte fixed header, the extension-header chain, Neighbor Discovery replacing ARP, SLAAC, Path MTU Discovery — is the IPv6 protocol episode.

One myth worth killing in this chapter, because it follows IPv6 around like a stray cat: IPv6 is not encrypted by default. IPsec was originally mandatory-to-implement in IPv6, but RFC 6434 in 2011 demoted it to optional. The encryption story for IPv6 is the same as for IPv4 — TLS at the application layer, covered in the TLS episode.

### The 50.1% Crossing — And Why It Inflected

On 28 March 2026, Google's IPv6 statistics dashboard recorded 50.1% for the first time. IPv6 briefly surpassed IPv4 in Google's measured user base. APNIC Labs and Cloudflare Radar still place global IPv6 capability in the 40 to 43 percent range — so the 50% number is a Google-specific snapshot, not a global tipping point. But it is a milestone the community has been waiting for since 1995.

What inflected it was mobile. T-Mobile US moved its mobile core to IPv6-only with 464XLAT — Cameron Byrne presented the production case at NANOG 61 in 2014, and that became the pattern every other carrier copied. Reliance Jio in India launched IPv6-first in 2016 and had more than 237 million IPv6 users by 2017 — the single biggest reason India's IPv6 share now runs between 67% and 80%. Meta runs more than 99% of its internal datacenter traffic over IPv6, and entire new clusters are IPv6-only. Meta has reported that internal IPv6 is 10 to 15% faster than IPv4, and on one carrier mobile measurement, 40% faster.

The 2026 numbers tell the same story. US mobile IPv6 averages around 87%. France sits at 86% in the Google February 2026 measurement. Germany is at 68%. China reports 865 million IPv6 users — 77% of users — carrying 34% of traffic, as of September 2025.

There was also an economic forcing function. AWS started charging half a cent per hour per public IPv4 address in February 2024. At scale, that turned IPv6-only architectures from an engineering curiosity into a cost line item. Combine that with 464XLAT shipping as a first-class citizen in modern Android, iOS 9 and later, macOS 13 and later, and Windows 11 — and IPv6-only access networks now Just Work for legacy IPv4 applications too.

### The KAME Turtle, And the 2024 RFC Backlog

The KAME Project ran from 1998 to March 2006 — a joint effort between Fujitsu, Hitachi, IIJ, NEC, Toshiba, Yokogawa, Keio University, and the University of Tokyo. It produced the free reference IPv6 and IPsec stack underneath FreeBSD, macOS, and iOS. The dancing turtle still appears at kame.net, but only if you reach it over IPv6. If you ever wondered where Apple's IPv6 stack came from, the answer is: a Japanese collaboration from 1998 named after a turtle. Apple's stack has long since moved past KAME, but the lineage is real.

The 2024 IETF backlog tells you where IPv6 work is happening today. RFC 9637, in August 2024, added a second documentation prefix — `3fff::/20` — on top of the older `2001:db8::/32`, large enough to model multi-AS networks for examples and labs. RFC 9673, in October 2024, finally relaxed Hop-by-Hop Options handling so those options are deployable on real router silicon instead of being treated as a performance grenade. RFC 9602, also in 2024, reserved `5f00::/16` for SRv6 segment IDs. And in a small but telling cleanup, the prefix `face:b00c` was removed from Linux kernel traces in 2024 because the example prefix from RFC 5514 was being mistaken for a real prefix in copy-pasted configs.

On the frontier: SoftBank, in December 2025, announced the world's first commercial 5G deployment using SRv6 MUP. Microsoft Azure Fairwater, presented at OCP 2025, uses SRv6 as fabric for what Microsoft calls the largest AI back-end network in the world. SRv6 is where the IPv6 story is going next.

And there is one more quiet forcing function worth naming. Apple's iCloud Private Relay, which launched in October 2021, prefers IPv6 egress when an AAAA record exists. Pure IPv4-only enterprise networks frequently break Private Relay — which means IPv6 deployment has become a compatibility requirement for any enterprise that wants Apple devices to behave correctly.

## The figures

### Steve Deering

Steve Deering, born 1951, is the architect of both IP multicast and IPv6. His 1991 Stanford PhD thesis invented IP multicast and gave us the Internet Group Management Protocol — the mechanism that powers IPTV, financial market data feeds, and intra-datacenter pub/sub today. Then he and Bob Hinden became the primary architects of IPv6 — RFC 1883 in 1995, RFC 2460 in 1998, and the current RFC 8200 in 2017. The simplified 40-byte header, the extension-header chain, the link-local addressing, the no-in-network-fragmentation rule — those are his design decisions. He won the IEEE Internet Award in 2010. On 28 March 2026, his spec finally crossed 50% of Google's traffic. The Steve Deering episode tells the full story.

### IPv6 Crosses 50% of Google's Traffic

On 28 March 2026, Google's IPv6 dashboard recorded a peak of 50.1% — 28 years after RFC 2460 was published. Cloudflare measured 40% of HTTP requests over IPv6; APNIC Labs measured 43.13% IPv6-capable networks. Same trend, different vantage points. The tipping mechanisms were AWS charging for public IPv4 in February 2024, 464XLAT shipping natively across modern OSes, and mobile carriers running IPv6-only cores. There is a separate entry on the Frontier page that walks through the metrics in detail.

### RFC 8200 — Internet Protocol, Version 6 (IPv6) Specification

Published in July 2017 by Steve Deering and Bob Hinden. STD 86 — the document that finally promoted IPv6 from Draft Standard to Internet Standard, 19 years after RFC 2460 and 22 years after the original RFC 1883. The on-the-wire protocol was essentially unchanged; the promotion was the IETF formally declaring the spec mature.

- **Before this chapter:** "IPv4" — the address space that ran out, and the NAT machinery that kept it alive long past its expiry date. That chapter is the prerequisite — IPv6 is the answer to the problem IPv4 created.
