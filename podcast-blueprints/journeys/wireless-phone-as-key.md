# A phone unlocks a car — BLE bootstrap, UWB ranging, and the unlock decision

## In one breath
A modern phone-as-car-key is a two-radio dance. Bluetooth Low Energy
discovers the car and exchanges credentials cheaply, and Ultra-Wideband
then proves the phone is physically next to the car using a distance
the speed of light cannot lie about. This journey walks the four steps
that turn a phone in your pocket into a key the door actually trusts.

## The hook (cold-open)
In 2022, a researcher unlocked a parked Tesla from twenty-five metres
away with about fifty dollars of Bluetooth dev boards. He was not
breaking the crypto. He was relaying the radio signal, and the car
believed the phone was right next to it. The CCC Digital Key 3.0
spec is the industry's answer to that attack. The trick is to keep
Bluetooth for what it is good at — discovery and credential exchange —
and to add a second radio whose physics make a relay impossible. We
are going to walk those four steps in order.

## The journey

### Step 1 — BLE advertising on channels 37, 38, and 39 (BLE)
The car's Bluetooth Low Energy radio broadcasts an `ADV_IND` packet on
the three advertising channels — 37 at 2402 MHz, 38 at 2426 MHz, and
39 at 2480 MHz — every hundred milliseconds or so. Those three
channels are picked deliberately to sit in the gaps between Wi-Fi
channels 1, 6, and 11. The advert carries the CCC Digital Key service
UUID and an ephemeral identifier. Your phone — an iPhone, a Galaxy, an
Android device running Aliro — is scanning those same channels
continuously. When the phone sees the car's UUID it sends a
`CONNECT_IND` back, both radios switch from the advertising channels to
one of the thirty-seven data channels, and the BLE link is up. The
full mechanism of how BLE advertising and connection setup works is in
the Bluetooth episode; here we just need to know the link exists.

The radio link is up, but a radio link by itself proves almost nothing.
A 2022 attack showed exactly how little: a relay box near the car and
another near the owner's pocket convinced a Tesla that the owner was
standing at the door. CCC Digital Key 3.0 fixes that with a layered
defence. The next step is the cryptographic half.

### Step 2 — GATT pairing and SPAKE2+ authentication (BLE)
Over the now-encrypted BLE channel, the car and the phone run a
SPAKE2-plus password-authenticated key exchange. The car proves it
holds the right vehicle key. The phone proves it holds the right
Digital Key applet inside its embedded Secure Element. The conversation
is a stream of EMV-style APDUs flowing over the GATT layer, carrying
the certificate chain in both directions. When the exchange finishes,
both sides share fresh session keys. Then comes the message that ties
the two radios together: the car sends the phone an `STS_KEY`, a
128-bit AES key that the upcoming Ultra-Wideband ranging session is
going to use. UWB does not have a power-efficient discovery mechanism
of its own, so BLE is the on-ramp — it bootstraps both the trust and
the key material UWB needs. The deeper details of GATT, SPAKE2-plus,
and how the secure element handles those APDUs live in the Bluetooth
episode.

The phone now has the STS_KEY. The car has the STS_KEY. Both sides
power up their Ultra-Wideband radios for a ranging round whose only
job is to prove that the phone is actually here, not relayed from
across the parking lot.

### Step 3 — UWB DS-TWR — three messages, six timestamps (UWB)
The phone transmits a Poll frame on UWB Channel 9, centred at
7987.2 MHz, with 499.2 MHz of bandwidth, in BPRF mode at 6.81 Mbps.
The frame carries a thirty-two-chip Scrambled Timestamp Sequence
generated by AES-128 in counter mode under the STS_KEY. That keyed
pulse pattern is the thing an attacker without the key cannot predict
or replay. The phone records `t1`, the transmit timestamp at the start-
of-frame delimiter, with about fifteen picoseconds of resolution. The
car — typically several anchors around the body — receive-timestamps
the Poll at `t2`, waits roughly two hundred microseconds, and transmits
a Response carrying `t2` and `t3`. The phone records `t4` on receive,
waits its own reply delay, and transmits a Final carrying `t1`, `t4`,
and `t5`. The car records `t6` on receive. All six timestamps now
exist, three on each side. The full mechanism — the impulse-radio
physics, the STS, the channelisation — is in the UWB episode.

The car now has everything it needs to compute time-of-flight. The
double-sided two-way formula is built specifically to cancel relative
clock drift to first order, so the cheap crystals on either side, off
by twenty parts per million, do not poison the answer. Multiply
time-of-flight by the speed of light and you have distance.

### Step 4 — Distance check and the unlock decision (UWB)
Using the DS-TWR cross-product — the round trips on each side multiplied
together, minus the reply delays multiplied together, divided by the
sum of all four — the car gets a time-of-flight figure good to about
one nanosecond. One nanosecond of light is roughly thirty centimetres.
Several anchors around the car triangulate that into an x, y, and z
position relative to the body. If the distance is inside the unlock
threshold, and the BLE credential checked out, and the STS validation
showed no Ghost-Peak-style injection on the air, the car unlocks the
side you are walking up to, runs the welcome animation, and adjusts
your seat. The single most important property of this whole exchange
is the one the relay attack cannot get around: time-of-flight cannot
be shortened. The speed of light is a hard upper bound. You can amplify
the signal, you can rebroadcast it, you can do whatever you like — you
cannot make the photons arrive earlier than light-speed allows. The
2022 BLE relay attack does not work against UWB ranging. And if the
phone's battery is dead, NFC is still there as the fallback — that
story is in the NFC episode and in the Tap to Pay journey.

## What the listener now understands
A phone-as-car-key is two radios doing two different jobs. Bluetooth
Low Energy is cheap, low-power, and good at discovery and credential
exchange — but on its own it cannot tell the car where the phone
actually is, and a relay attack proved that the hard way. Ultra-
Wideband is expensive in silicon and power, but it can measure
time-of-flight at picosecond resolution, and physics will not let an
attacker cheat that measurement. Stack the two together and you get
the principle the CCC Digital Key 3.0 spec is built on: cryptography
proves who, and physics proves where.
