# Irwin Jacobs

## In one sentence
Irwin Jacobs is the MIT-trained engineer who co-founded Qualcomm in 1985, then spent the next decade fighting the rest of the cellular industry to prove that Code Division Multiple Access could actually carry a phone call — and won the argument so completely that the GSM camp eventually adopted his math for 3G.

## The hook (cold-open)
In January 1989, the Cellular Telecommunications Industry Association sat through a technical presentation on CDMA and then voted against it. The line that came out of the room afterwards was striking: no one had found a hole in the technical case. They had voted on politics, not physics. The man whose company had given that presentation, Irwin Jacobs of Qualcomm, then spent the next ten years pushing CDMA into the network anyway — first in Hong Kong in 1995, then Korea, then the United States. By the time 3G arrived, the GSM world had folded CDMA's mathematics into its own standard. The vote had aged badly.

## The work

### MIT, the textbook, and Linkabit
Jacobs was trained at MIT and, in 1965, co-authored Principles of Communication Engineering with John Wozencraft. The book is still in print sixty years later — that detail matters because it tells you who Jacobs was before he was a CEO. He was a communication-theory academic with a textbook on his shelf that other engineers were learning from. In 1968 he co-founded Linkabit with Andrew Viterbi, the inventor of the Viterbi algorithm — there is a separate episode on Viterbi. Linkabit was the warm-up act: a small communications-engineering shop that put the two of them in the room with the right military and satellite contracts to learn what a commercial spread-spectrum business could look like.

### Qualcomm, 1985
In 1985 Jacobs co-founded Qualcomm and ran it as CEO for the next twenty years, until 2005. The early Qualcomm bet was that the right way to share a cellular radio channel was not to slice it in time, the way the TDMA-based GSM camp wanted to, but to spread every call across the whole channel using orthogonal codes — Code Division Multiple Access. The technical argument was about capacity: CDMA, Qualcomm claimed, would let the same spectrum carry more calls.

### The 1989 CTIA fight
The political argument was the harder one. The CTIA's January 1989 vote against CDMA — the "no one found a hole in the technical presentation" moment — set the tone for what followed. The cellular industry had already standardised on TDMA in Europe as GSM, and the US carriers were leaning the same way. Jacobs and Qualcomm spent the next decade running a commercial fight: building handsets and base stations, signing carriers one at a time, and proving the capacity claim in live networks rather than in committee.

### cdmaOne ships — Hong Kong, Korea, the United States
The first commercial cdmaOne network went live in Hong Kong in 1995. Korea followed and made CDMA the backbone of its national mobile build-out. The United States came after that. By the late 1990s CDMA was no longer a thought experiment; it was a working alternative to GSM with real subscribers on it.

### The fork closes — WCDMA in UMTS
The decisive win came when 3G was being designed. The mathematical foundation of Qualcomm's CDMA became the basis of WCDMA, the air interface inside the UMTS standard — which is to say the GSM camp adopted the technology they had voted against in 1989. From there the family flows directly into the modern 3GPP radio stack, the protocol covered in the cellular episode: the LTE and 5G New Radio system that gives a phone an IP address from a base station fifty kilometres away, that runs roughly nine billion subscriptions, and that is the largest wireless deployment on Earth.

## Awards and recognition
Jacobs received the National Medal of Technology in 1994 — the United States' top honour for technological achievement — and the IEEE Alexander Graham Bell Medal in 1995, a year after cdmaOne was about to launch in Hong Kong. The two awards arrived on either side of the decision that proved CDMA was real.

## Quotes
On the January 1989 CTIA vote against CDMA, Jacobs's own gloss is the line that has travelled: "no one found a hole in the technical presentation." It is quoted because it captures, in one sentence, what the next ten years of his career were about — winning a fight that had already been won on the engineering and lost on the politics.
