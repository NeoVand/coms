# Charles Walton

## In one sentence
Charles Walton was the IBM disk-drive engineer who left to found a one-man Sunnyvale shop in 1970, filed the patent that first put the word "RFID" on paper, and lived just long enough to see his idea ride into a billion phones as NFC.

## The hook (cold-open)
On 15 May 1983, the US Patent Office issued patent number 4,384,288 to a 61-year-old engineer named Charles Walton. The title was "Portable Radio Frequency Emitting Identifier." It was the first patent ever to use the acronym RFID. Twenty-eight years later, three months after Google shipped its first tap-to-pay wallet on the Nexus S 4G, Walton died in Los Gatos at 89 — by then his patents had expired, the royalties had stopped, and the technology he had named was about to land in every pocket on earth.

## The work

### From Cornell to the Signal Corps to IBM
Walton took his bachelor's in electrical engineering at Cornell in 1943, picked up a master's at Stevens Institute of Technology, served in the US Army Signal Corps, and spent ten years at IBM working primarily on disk-drive research and development. The disk-drive years matter for what came next: he was a hardware engineer used to thinking about how a small, passive object — a sector on a platter — could be addressed and read at a distance.

### Proximity Devices, 1970
In 1970 he left IBM and founded Proximity Devices in Sunnyvale. He spent the next two decades — through his fifties and into his sixties — inventing the radio-identification primitives that would later become the protocol family we cover in the NFC episode. He filed his earliest passive transponder patent, US 3,752,960, in August 1973. By the end he held more than fifty patents in radio identification.

### The 1983 patent and the Schlage deal
The canonical patent is US 4,384,288, "Portable Radio Frequency Emitting Identifier," issued 15 May 1983. It is the first patent in the historical record to use the term RFID. The product Walton actually shipped from it was a card-and-reader door lock, and he licensed that lock to Schlage. The Schlage royalties earned him millions over the following decade.

### The timing problem
The cruel arithmetic of his career is in the dates. The bulk of Walton's patents expired in the mid-1990s. Walmart's RFID supplier mandate landed in 2003. The first tap-to-pay phones arrived at the end of the 2000s. Google Wallet 1.0 launched on the Nexus S 4G in 2011 — and Walton died in Los Gatos on 6 November 2011, three months later, at 89. He had been right about the technology and a quarter-century early on the market.

## Awards and recognition
The Lemelson-MIT Program, which honours American inventors, recognised Walton for the RFID work. The dump records no other formal awards; the canonical contemporary citations are the VentureBeat and Engadget obituaries that ran in November 2011.
