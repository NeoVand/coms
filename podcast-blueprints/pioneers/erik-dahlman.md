# Erik Dahlman

## In one sentence
Erik Dahlman is the Ericsson Research engineer who helped design the LTE Release 8 physical layer and the 5G NR numerology that together carry the radio traffic of roughly nine billion cellular subscriptions.

## The hook (cold-open)
Two reference textbooks sit on the desk of essentially every cellular engineer who came of age in the 2010s and 2020s. One is the LTE book. One is the 5G NR book. Both have Erik Dahlman's name on the cover. He did not just write about these systems from the outside — he was inside Ericsson Research helping design them, and inside 3GPP helping standardise them, release after release.

## The work

### LTE Release 8 and the 4G physical layer
Dahlman's first defining contribution was as a personal contributor to the LTE Release 8 PHY — the physical layer of 4G LTE, frozen in 2008 and rolled out across the world's mobile networks through the early 2010s. The mechanism — how OFDM subcarriers, resource blocks, and the LTE frame structure get a phone an IP address from a base station tens of kilometres away — belongs to the cellular episode. What matters here is that Dahlman was one of the engineers in the room when those choices were made.

### 5G NR numerology
The second defining contribution is the numerology of 5G NR — the scalable subcarrier-spacing scheme that lets the same air interface serve a low-band wide-area cell and a millimetre-wave small cell from one design. Again, the mechanism is for the cellular episode; the point for a biographical pass is that Dahlman was one of the systems architects who shaped how 5G's radio actually breathes across bands and use cases.

### The reference textbooks
With his Ericsson Research colleagues Stefan Parkvall and Johan Sköld, Dahlman co-authored the two canonical reference texts that every cellular engineer of the era has read or cited. *4G LTE/LTE-Advanced for Mobile Broadband*, published by Academic Press across multiple editions through 2014, became the standard description of 4G. *5G NR: The Next Generation Wireless Access Technology*, first edition 2018 and second edition 2020, did the same job for 5G. Few standards-track engineers manage to also write the textbooks that everyone else uses to learn the standard; Dahlman did it twice.

### 3GPP TSG-RAN, every release since LTE
Dahlman has been active in 3GPP TSG-RAN — the working group that owns the radio access network — through every release since LTE. The shorthand inside that community is that his name on a study item means the study is real. That kind of long, sustained, release-by-release contribution is what turns a standards body's output into deployable infrastructure.
