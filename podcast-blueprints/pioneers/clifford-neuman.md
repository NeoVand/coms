# Clifford Neuman

## In one sentence
Clifford Neuman is one of the principal architects of Kerberos V5, the trusted-third-party authentication protocol that, four decades on, still guards every Windows domain, every Hadoop cluster, and every secured NFSv4 mount on Earth.

## The hook (cold-open)
In the late 1980s, MIT's Project Athena had a problem you would recognise today: thousands of users, thousands of workstations, and no good way to prove who was who without sending a password across the wire. The answer was Kerberos — a three-headed dog from Greek mythology, a trusted third party that hands out tickets instead of passing passwords. Clifford Neuman was one of the people who designed the version that stuck. Kerberos V5 became RFC 4120, and it is the silent authentication layer behind a startling fraction of the enterprise software stack.

## The work

### Kerberos V5 — MIT Project Athena and RFC 4120
Neuman joined MIT Project Athena in the late 1980s during the Kerberos design phase and became one of the principal architects of Kerberos V5, eventually standardised as RFC 4120. The mechanism — tickets, a key distribution centre, mutual authentication without ever sending the password — belongs to the Kerberos episode. What belongs in the biography is the scale of what shipped: every Active Directory domain in the world speaks Kerberos, and the same protocol underpins authenticated access in Hadoop, NFSv4, and a long tail of Unix services.

### The 1994 paper with Theodore Ts'o
In 1994, Neuman co-authored the canonical paper *Kerberos: An Authentication Service for Computer Networks* with Theodore Ts'o, published in IEEE Communications Magazine. That article — not the RFC — is how a generation of engineers actually learned how Kerberos works. Plenty of working systems administrators have a printed copy of it in a binder somewhere.

### USC Information Sciences Institute and the GOST project
Neuman has been a senior research scientist at USC Information Sciences Institute for three decades. He completed his PhD at the University of Washington in 1992, on naming and security in distributed systems, and at ISI he led the GOST project — Gigabit Operating System Technologies — and has continued to publish on distributed-systems security from there.

### Network Security, the textbook
With Charlie Kaufman and Radia Perlman, Neuman is co-author of the textbook *Network Security: Private Communication in a Public World*. There is a separate episode on Radia Perlman. The book is the one that connects Kerberos, IPsec, TLS, and PKI into a single coherent pedagogy — it is the standard text for engineers who need to understand all four at once instead of one at a time. The TLS episode and the IPsec material cover the protocols themselves.
