# Larry W. Fullerton

## In one sentence
Larry Fullerton is the Huntsville engineer who founded Time Domain Corporation in 1987, filed the foundational patents on impulse-radio modulation and time-hopping codes, and built the company whose patent stack underpinned the early-2000s ultra-wideband licensing landscape that we now feel as AirTag Precision Finding and the BMW Digital Key.

## The hook (cold-open)
In 1987, in Huntsville, Alabama, an engineer named Larry Fullerton founded a company called Time Domain Corporation around a single idea: instead of modulating a continuous carrier, transmit sub-nanosecond impulses across several gigahertz of spectrum at once and read the time-of-flight. Over the following decade he and his co-authors filed the patents on impulse-radio modulation, time-hopping codes, and pulse-position encoding that would define the field. When the FCC opened the 3.1 to 10.6 gigahertz band to ultra-wideband in 2002, his patent portfolio was sitting there waiting for it.

## The work

### Time Domain Corporation, Huntsville, 1987
Fullerton founded Time Domain Corporation in Huntsville, Alabama in 1987. From the start it was the canonical 1990s ultra-wideband start-up: patent-heavy, technically influential well beyond the size of its products. The protocol mechanics — sub-nanosecond impulses across several gigahertz, time-of-flight ranging to ten or thirty centimetres — belong to the ultra-wideband episode; what matters here is that Time Domain was the company that filed the foundational US patents on impulse-radio modulation, time-hopping codes, and pulse-position encoding through the late 1980s and 1990s.

### The early-1990s impulse-radio papers
On the academic side, Fullerton co-authored the early-1990s impulse-radio papers with Moe Z. Win and Robert A. Scholtz — work like *"Time-hopping SSMA techniques for impulse radio with an analog modulated data subcarrier,"* presented at the IEEE Spread Spectrum Techniques Symposium in 1996. These papers, alongside the patents, are the technical groundwork that everyone building ultra-wideband in the 2000s and 2010s ended up reading or licensing. There are separate pioneer episodes on Win and on Scholtz that pick up the academic side of that story.

### The pivot to defence and metrology, and Humatics
Through the 2000s, as the consumer ultra-wideband market took longer to arrive than anyone in 1999 expected, Time Domain pivoted toward military and intelligence-grade radar and toward precision metrology. The company eventually became Humatics. Fullerton's original patents underpinned much of the early-2000s ultra-wideband licensing landscape and the early FCC proceedings that opened the 3.1 to 10.6 gigahertz band in 2002 — the band that the modern consumer chips from Apple, NXP, and Qorvo all live inside today.
