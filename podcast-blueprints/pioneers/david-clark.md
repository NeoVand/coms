# David D. Clark

## In one sentence
David Clark is the engineer who served as Chief Protocol Architect of the internet through the 1980s and then, in one sentence at an IETF plenary in 1992, gave the standards body its motto.

## The hook (cold-open)
July 1992, Cambridge, Massachusetts. The IETF has gathered for its 24th meeting, and the question on the table is whether the internet will run on the OSI stack the world's telecoms ministries are pushing, or on TCP/IP. David Clark steps up to give a talk called "A Cloudy Crystal Ball." One sentence in it travels for the next thirty years. "We reject: kings, presidents and voting. We believe in: rough consensus and running code." The OSI stack does not win.

## The work

### Chief Protocol Architect, 1981 to 1989
From 1981 to 1989, Clark held the title Chief Protocol Architect of the internet. The decade matters: most of the architectural decisions that shape the internet today were made on his watch. The end-to-end principle — keep the network simple, push complexity to the edges. The four-layer model that organises the stack the TCP episode and the IPv4 episode walk through. The separation of mechanism from policy that lets the same protocols carry traffic for parties who disagree about almost everything else. We won't unpack the mechanisms here; the chapter on "What Is a Protocol?" and the chapter on "The Layer Model" each pick up the architecture in its own context. The biographical fact is that Clark was the person sitting in the architect's chair while those decisions were made.

### "A Cloudy Crystal Ball," IETF 24, July 1992
The talk was a working-group keynote, not a paper. The line about kings, presidents and voting was a description of how the IETF already worked, distilled into a sentence the room could repeat. It became the closest thing the IETF has to a national anthem, and it landed in the middle of the standards war that the chapter on "The OSI vs TCP/IP War" tells in full. Clark is still at MIT CSAIL, still working.

## Awards and recognition
Clark received the IEEE Internet Award in 1990, recognising the architectural work of the 1980s.

## Quotes
"We reject: kings, presidents and voting. We believe in: rough consensus and running code." From "A Cloudy Crystal Ball," delivered at IETF 24 in July 1992. The line travelled because it was true on the day he said it and has stayed true since — the IETF still settles questions by humming in a room, and the running code still has to exist before the standard is finished.
