# Robert A. Scholtz

## In one sentence
Robert Scholtz is the USC professor who, in a 1993 military-communications paper, defined the time-hopping spread-spectrum framework that lets many users share an ultra-wideband channel — the theoretical seed of every modern UWB system, from the AirTag's U1 chip to BMW's Digital Key Plus.

## The hook (cold-open)
In October 1993, at the IEEE military-communications conference in Boston, a USC professor named Robert Scholtz presented a paper titled "Multiple-Access with Time-Hopping Impulse Modulation." The room was small. The technology it described — sub-nanosecond radio pulses, scattered in time so that many devices could share the same enormous slice of spectrum — was not legal to transmit in the United States for almost another decade. Today the framework in that paper sits inside the U1 chip in an Apple AirTag, the radio in a BMW Digital Key, and the hands-free unlock in the Aliro standard. The technology had to wait twenty years for the silicon and the regulations to catch up to it.

## The work

### The 1993 MILCOM paper
The canonical citation is R. A. Scholtz, "Multiple-Access with Time-Hopping Impulse Modulation," in the proceedings of IEEE MILCOM 1993, Boston, 11 to 14 October 1993. The paper's contribution is the time-hopping spread-spectrum framework: instead of modulating a continuous carrier, an impulse-radio transmitter sends very short pulses, and a per-user pseudo-random hopping code decides which time slots its pulses land in. Different users hop on different codes, so the collisions average out and many devices share the same wide band. The mechanism — the sub-nanosecond pulses, the 6-to-9-gigahertz band, the time-of-flight ranging that gets you to ten or thirty centimetres — is the subject of the UWB episode; the biography point is that the multi-user framework everything else builds on came out of this paper.

### Win and Scholtz, 2000
With his student Moe Z. Win, Scholtz co-authored "Ultra-Wide Bandwidth Time-Hopping Spread-Spectrum Impulse Radio for Wireless Multiple-Access Communications" in the IEEE Transactions on Communications, April 2000. It is the most-cited UWB physical-layer paper in the field. The FiRa Consortium — the industry group behind today's UWB ranging standards — cites it explicitly as foundational in its own *Introduction to Impulse Radio UWB* white paper. The Engineering and Technology History Wiki entry for Scholtz singles the same paper out.

### What was downstream
Everything that ships UWB today rides on the framework Scholtz set down in the 1990s. The AirTag's Precision Finding mode uses time-of-flight on impulse-radio pulses to walk a user the last few metres to a lost item. BMW Digital Key Plus uses the same physics to know whether the phone is in the driver's pocket or on the pavement next to the car. The Aliro standard takes it into hands-free building access. The mechanism story belongs to the UWB episode; the point for this biography is that the theorist of the multi-user impulse-radio channel was a USC professor who published the seed papers a full decade before the FCC opened the band for unlicensed use.
