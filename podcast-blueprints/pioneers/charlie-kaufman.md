# Charlie Kaufman

## In one sentence
Charlie Kaufman is the engineer who edited IKEv2 across its full twenty-year lifecycle, from the original 2005 RFC through to the 2014 Internet Standard that anchors every modern IPsec deployment in the world.

## The hook (cold-open)
Every site-to-site VPN your company runs, every mobile-core backhaul carrying 3GPP traffic, and every built-in VPN client on macOS, iOS, Windows, and Android has the same handshake protocol underneath it: IKEv2. One person's name sits on the editor line of all three of its defining RFCs — the 2005 original, the 2010 clarifications, and the 2014 Internet Standard. That person is Charlie Kaufman, and he did it while moving between five of the most consequential systems shops of the era.

## The work

### Editor of IKEv2 — RFC 4306, RFC 5996, RFC 7296
Kaufman's central contribution is IKEv2, the Internet Key Exchange protocol that negotiates the cryptographic keys IPsec uses to protect traffic. He was the lead editor on RFC 4306 in 2005 — the original specification — then on RFC 5996 in 2010, which folded in clarifications and errata, and finally on RFC 7296 in 2014, which the IETF promoted to an Internet Standard. RFC 7296 is STD 79. The handshake mechanics belong to the IPsec episode; what matters for the biography is that one editor carried the same protocol through three RFCs and twenty years of deployment without losing the thread.

### Network Security: Private Communication in a Public World
Kaufman is co-author of *Network Security: Private Communication in a Public World*, published by Prentice Hall in multiple editions. It is the textbook that taught a generation of engineers how to think about cryptographic protocols — what an authentication exchange actually is, why naive designs leak, and how the pieces compose. It sits on the same shelf as the canonical security textbooks and is still cited in graduate courses.

### Career across DEC, Lotus, Iris, IBM, Microsoft
Kaufman's career moved through Digital Equipment Corporation, Lotus, Iris Associates — where he worked on the security architecture of Lotus Notes — IBM after the Lotus acquisition, and finally Microsoft. The thread across all of it is the same: pragmatic security engineering inside production systems, with a parallel life in the IETF as the kind of editor whose name on a draft means it actually works in the field.
