# Marty Cooper

## In one sentence
Marty Cooper is the Motorola engineer who, on a Manhattan sidewalk on 3 April 1973, placed the first public handheld cellular phone call — and dialled it to the rival at AT&T Bell Labs whose own team he had just beaten to it.

## The hook (cold-open)
On 3 April 1973, Marty Cooper stood on Sixth Avenue in Manhattan, raised a two-and-a-half-pound prototype called the DynaTAC to his ear, and dialled Joel Engel at AT&T Bell Labs. *"Joel, this is Marty. I'm calling you from a cell phone, a real handheld portable cell phone."* Engel ran AT&T's competing cellular programme. The phone Cooper was holding offered thirty-five minutes of talk time after a ten-hour charge. Fifty-three years later, the radio family it seeded carries roughly nine billion subscriptions.

## The work

### Motorola and the DynaTAC team, 1973
Cooper joined Motorola in 1954 and stayed for nearly three decades. By the early 1970s he was running the team that built the DynaTAC — the prototype that would settle a question AT&T and Motorola had been arguing for years: should the cellular future be a car phone, wired into a vehicle's electrical system, or a true handheld device that a person carried? Cooper's team built the handheld and, on 3 April 1973, took it out onto the street to prove it worked. The first public call went from Sixth Avenue to Bell Labs in New Jersey. The choice of recipient was not subtle. Joel Engel was leading the rival AT&T effort.

The hardware was unforgiving. The DynaTAC weighed two and a half pounds, gave thirty-five minutes of talk time, and needed ten hours on the charger to deliver them. As Cooper put it later: *"The battery lifetime wasn't really a problem because you couldn't hold that phone up for that long!"*

### The patent and the legacy
The "radio telephone system" patent application was filed on 17 October 1973, six months after the demonstration call. The handheld concept it described is the one that won. The car phone lost. The 3GPP radio family covered in the cellular episode — 4G LTE and 5G NR — carries that lineage forward into roughly nine billion subscriptions, the largest wireless deployment on Earth.

Cooper left Motorola in 1983 and went on to found Dyna LLC. At 96 he is still active in spectrum-policy advocacy.

## Awards and recognition
Cooper received the Charles Stark Draper Prize in 2013 — the National Academy of Engineering's top honour — and the IEEE Masaru Ibuka Consumer Electronics Award in 2015. He is widely referred to as the father of the handheld cell phone.

## Quotes
