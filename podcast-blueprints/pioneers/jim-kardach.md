# Jim Kardach

## In one sentence
Jim Kardach is the Intel engineer who, on a flight in 1997 with a paperback of *The Long Ships* in his lap, suggested calling the new short-range radio standard "Bluetooth" — as a placeholder, until marketing thought of something better, which they never did.

## The hook (cold-open)
In 1997, five companies — Ericsson, IBM, Intel, Nokia, and Toshiba — were trying to agree on a single short-range wireless standard, and they did not yet have a name for it. An Intel engineer named Jim Kardach was reading Frans G. Bengtsson's Viking novel *The Long Ships* and noticed the analogy: a 10th-century Danish king named Harald "Blåtand" Gormsson had united Denmark and Norway, and the SIG was trying to do the same thing to the wireless industry. He proposed the king's name as a placeholder. Twenty-eight years and several billion radios later, it is still the name.

## The work

### The 1997 SIG planning meeting
Kardach was at Intel when the Bluetooth Special Interest Group was being assembled. At a 1997 SIG planning meeting, he proposed naming the project after Harald Blåtand Gormsson — the 10th-century Danish king who had united Denmark and Norway — on the explicit grounds that the SIG was trying to unite Ericsson, IBM, Intel, Nokia, and Toshiba behind one wireless standard. The name was meant to be temporary. Marketing was supposed to replace it with something more product-ready before launch. They never did.

### The May 1998 founding charter
Beyond the name, Kardach drove much of Intel's early Bluetooth silicon strategy and was instrumental in landing the May 1998 SIG founding charter — the document that put the five founder companies behind a single radio specification. The protocol mechanics — Classic BR/EDR, BLE, the 2.4 GHz band, the two stacks for streaming audio versus low-power sensors — belong to the Bluetooth episode. What Kardach shipped at this stage was the institutional alignment: getting five competitors to sign the same piece of paper.

### The logo, as a bind-rune
The Bluetooth logo came out of the same original branding work. It is a bind-rune — Harald's initials in Younger Futhark, the runes Hagall (ᚼ) and Bjarkan (ᛒ) overlaid on each other. The Viking-king conceit, in other words, was not a one-off naming joke; it was carried straight into the mark that now appears on every certified device.

## Quotes

> "Bluetooth was originally intended as a placeholder name, until marketing could come up with something better. They never did."

Kardach wrote this in his EETimes column "The Naming of a Technology," the canonical first-person account of how the name was chosen. The line travels because it inverts the usual order of branding stories — the placeholder won, and the marketing department's better idea never arrived.
