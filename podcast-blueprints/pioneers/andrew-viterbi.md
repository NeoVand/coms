# Andrew Viterbi

## In one sentence
Andrew Viterbi is the engineer who, in 1967, wrote a single decoding algorithm that now runs inside every cellular phone, every disk-drive read channel, every GPS receiver, and every speech recognizer on Earth — and then co-founded the company that turned CDMA into the global cellular standard.

## The hook (cold-open)
In 1967, Andrew Viterbi published an algorithm for decoding convolutional codes. On the advice of a lawyer, he did not patent it. The algorithm was named after him anyway, and six decades later it sits inside the radio of the phone in your pocket, the read channel of the SSD it talks to, the GPS chip that tells it where it is, and the speech recognizer that hears you. It made him nothing directly. It made Qualcomm — the company he co-founded eighteen years later — everything.

## The work

### The Viterbi algorithm — 1967
The 1967 paper gave a maximum-likelihood way to decode a convolutional code by walking a trellis and keeping only the surviving paths. We won't unpack the mechanism here — what matters for the biography is the deployment list. Every digital cellular system uses it. Every modern disk-drive read channel uses it. Every GPS receiver uses it to recover the navigation message. Speech recognizers use the same dynamic-programming structure for hidden Markov models. It is one of the most widely instantiated pieces of mathematics in production communications. Viterbi did not patent it.

### Linkabit — 1968
A year after the algorithm, Viterbi co-founded Linkabit with Irwin Jacobs — there's a separate episode on him — and Leonard Kleinrock. Linkabit became the seed company for a generation of San Diego communications engineers; many of the people who later staffed Qualcomm came through it.

### Qualcomm — 1 July 1985
Viterbi and Jacobs co-founded Qualcomm on 1 July 1985. The bet the company made was on CDMA — code-division multiple access — as the basis for digital cellular, against the prevailing TDMA orthodoxy. CDMA shipped as the IS-95 standard in the 1990s, and the spread-spectrum and coding ideas behind it carried into the 3GPP cellular family that followed. The cellular episode covers the radio mechanism; the takeaway for the biography is that the company turned a research curiosity into the technology underneath roughly nine billion subscriptions and the largest wireless deployment on Earth.

### The USC gift — 2004
In 2004 Viterbi gave $52 million to the University of Southern California's engineering school. It is now the USC Viterbi School of Engineering. The detail to hold onto is the symmetry: the algorithm that he chose not to patent paid out, eventually, through the company that depended on it.

## Awards and recognition
Viterbi received the Marconi Prize in 1990, the National Medal of Science in 2007, and the IEEE Medal of Honor in 2010 — the IEEE's highest award, given for the algorithm and for the body of work in digital communications that followed.
