# Skip Ashton

## In one sentence
Skip Ashton is the engineer who led EmberZNet PRO — the reference Zigbee stack that ended up inside the Hue bulbs, Trådfri lights, and Walmart shelf labels of the world — and then carried that authority through two acquisitions and onto the boards of both 802.15.4 mesh standards.

## The hook (cold-open)
There is one piece of software that every other Zigbee stack on Earth gets benchmarked against, and it came out of a small Cambridge, Massachusetts company called Ember Corporation. The man who ran engineering on that stack is Skip Ashton. When Silicon Labs bought Ember in May 2012, Skip stayed on and took over software for the combined company's 8-bit and 32-bit microcontrollers. He has since moved on to Infineon as a Distinguished Engineer — and he is one of the very few people who sits on the technical leadership of both 802.15.4 mesh stacks at once: Zigbee, and Thread.

## The work

### Ember Corporation and EmberZNet PRO
Ember Corporation was founded in 2001 in Cambridge, Massachusetts, by Andrew Wheeler and Robert Poor. Skip joined as VP of Engineering and Technology, and the work he is most identified with is **EmberZNet PRO** — Ember's Zigbee stack. The mechanism story belongs to the Zigbee episode; what matters here is that EmberZNet PRO became the reference implementation, the stack that every other Zigbee implementation is still measured against, and the radio software inside an enormous fraction of the Philips Hue bulbs, IKEA Trådfri lights, and Walmart shelf labels deployed around the world.

### Silicon Labs, May 2012
Silicon Labs acquired Ember in May 2012. Skip became VP of Software at Silicon Labs, with a portfolio that grew well past Zigbee — he covered drivers for the company's 8-bit and 32-bit microcontrollers, EmberZNet itself, and short-range sub-gigahertz radio software.

### Standards work — Zigbee Alliance and beyond
Skip has been involved with Zigbee since 2004. He served on the Zigbee Alliance board, and chaired both the **Zigbee Technical Committee** and the **Zigbee Test and Certification Committee** — the two committees that decide what the protocol does and what counts as a conforming implementation. He also served on the NIST Smart Grid Architecture Committee during the period when 802.15.4-based mesh was being threaded into utility infrastructure.

### Infineon, and the Thread Group
He later moved to Infineon as a Distinguished Engineer, and has served on the **Thread Group board** as well. Thread is the other major 802.15.4 mesh stack — the one underneath Matter on the home-automation side. Holding technical authority over both Zigbee and Thread at the same time is rare, and it is the through-line of his career: the radio is 802.15.4, and Skip has been in the room for both stacks built on top of it.
