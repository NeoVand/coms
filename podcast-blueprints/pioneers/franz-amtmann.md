# Franz Amtmann

## In one sentence
Franz Amtmann is the Austrian electrical engineer who, across more than twenty-five years at Philips Semiconductors and its spin-out NXP, led the physical-layer and integrated-circuit work that turned NFC from a paper draft into the manufacturable silicon now sitting in billions of phones, payment terminals, and transit gates.

## The hook (cold-open)
In June 2015 the European Patent Office handed its Industry-category European Inventor Award to two engineers — a Frenchman, Philippe Maugars, and an Austrian, Franz Amtmann — for jointly inventing NFC. Amtmann had spent more than two decades at one company at the same site in Gratkorn, Austria, first under the Philips Semiconductors banner and then, after the 2006 spin-out, under NXP. By the time the award arrived, his name was on roughly fifty patents, and the protocol he had helped get into silicon was the one your phone now uses to pay for coffee.

## The work

### Philips Semiconductors, NXP, and the Gratkorn site
Amtmann's career is a single-employer story. He spent more than twenty-five years at Philips Semiconductors and then NXP, the semiconductor business that Philips spun out in 2006. The work was based at NXP's site in Gratkorn, Austria, which became one of the centres of gravity for the company's contactless and identification silicon. That continuity matters: the NFC physical layer and the MIFARE family were not done as a one-off invention, they were done as a multi-decade engineering programme inside a single integrated-circuit organisation.

### MIFARE and the NFC physical layer
Amtmann's central contribution is on the silicon side of the standard. He led the physical-layer work behind NFC and was instrumental in the architecture of the MIFARE family — the contactless smart-card platform that runs much of the world's transit ticketing and access control. The protocol mechanism — the 13.56-megahertz inductively-coupled radio at roughly four centimetres of range, the payments, transit, identity, access, and tap-to-pair commissioning modes — belongs to the NFC episode. What matters here is the production: Amtmann is the engineer who took the paper draft of the standard and turned it into manufacturable integrated circuits at scale.

### The 2015 award and the partnership with Maugars
The European Patent Office's 2015 European Inventor Award, in the Industry category, went jointly to Amtmann and Philippe Maugars — there is a separate episode on Maugars — and it was given specifically for jointly inventing NFC. In a 2015 interview with M2M Now, the two of them credited the success to, in their words, "a cooperation between teams having complementary expertise across RFID, applications, and IC design." That sentence is the whole shape of the project: a wireless air-interface standard does not become a billion-unit shipping product without the chip people, the application people, and the radio-identification people all sitting at the same table.

## Quotes
On the joint award, in the 2015 M2M Now interview, Amtmann and Maugars together described the success as "a cooperation between teams having complementary expertise across RFID, applications, and IC design" — a deliberate framing that gave the credit to cross-disciplinary engineering teams rather than to a single moment of invention.
