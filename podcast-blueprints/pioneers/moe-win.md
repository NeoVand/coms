# Moe Z. Win

## In one sentence
Moe Win is the wireless-systems researcher whose decade of papers on ultra-wideband ranging in cluttered, multi-path environments became the theoretical foundation that every modern UWB chip — the one in the AirTag, the one behind BMW Digital Key, the one inside Aliro hands-free unlock — quietly relies on.

## The hook (cold-open)
Open an AirTag, look at the U1 chip, and ask why the centimetre-accurate distance estimate it produces actually works inside a living room full of furniture and walls. The answer leads back to a 2009 paper in the Proceedings of the IEEE, written by Moe Win and four co-authors, that wrote down the canonical multipath bias model for ultra-wideband time-of-arrival ranging. Before MIT, Win had spent five years at AT&T Research Laboratories and seven at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. By the time the consumer UWB wave arrived, he was the Robert R. Taylor Professor at MIT and the founding director of the Wireless Information and Network Sciences Laboratory in LIDS.

## The work

### From Texas A&M and USC to JPL and AT&T
Win took his bachelor's degree at Texas A&M, then a master's in applied mathematics and a PhD in electrical engineering at the University of Southern California. His doctoral advisor was Robert A. Scholtz at USC's Communication Sciences Institute — there is a separate episode on Scholtz, who is the theoretical grandfather of impulse-radio UWB. Win spent seven years at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and then five at AT&T Research Laboratories before moving to MIT.

### The MIT WINS Lab
At MIT, Win is the Robert R. Taylor Professor in the Laboratory for Information and Decision Systems, and the founding director of the Wireless Information and Network Sciences Laboratory inside it — the WINS Lab. The lab is the institutional vehicle for the long programme of work on UWB localisation, network experimentation, and high-accuracy ranging that the consumer products eventually shipped on top of.

### The 2009 Proceedings of the IEEE paper
The single piece of work the rest of the field most consistently cites is the February 2009 Proceedings of the IEEE paper, "Ranging With Ultrawide Bandwidth Signals in Multipath Environments," co-authored with Davide Dardari, Andrea Conti, Ulric Ferner, and Andrea Giorgetti. It is the canonical reference for UWB time-of-arrival ranging theory in multipath. The protocol mechanism — the sub-nanosecond pulses at six to nine gigahertz, the double-sided two-way ranging exchange, the Scrambled Timestamp Sequence — belongs to the UWB episode. What matters here is the production: every modern double-sided two-way ranging implementation owes its multipath bias model to this paper.

## Awards and recognition
Win is an IEEE Fellow. In 2006 he received the IEEE Eric E. Sumner Award, with a citation reading "for pioneering contributions to ultra-wide band communications science and technology." He has also received the IEEE Kiyo Tomiyasu Award.

## Where they appear in the book
- Part "Wireless," chapter "UWB — nanosecond pulses for centimetre ranging" — the chapter on the sub-nanosecond impulse-radio that puts AirTag Precision Finding, BMW Digital Key, and Aliro hands-free unlock onto the same physical layer. Win's multipath ranging theory is the academic spine the chapter rests on.

## See also (other pioneer episodes)
The natural companion is the Robert Scholtz episode — Scholtz was Win's USC doctoral advisor and the theoretical founder of time-hopping impulse radio, and the two of them co-authored the most-cited UWB physical-layer paper, "Ultra-Wide Bandwidth Time-Hopping Spread-Spectrum Impulse Radio for Wireless Multiple-Access Communications," in the IEEE Transactions on Communications in April 2000. After the Scholtz and Win pair, the next thing to play is the UWB protocol episode itself, which covers the ranging mechanics their theory enabled.
