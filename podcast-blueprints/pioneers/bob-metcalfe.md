# Bob Metcalfe

## In one sentence
Bob Metcalfe is the engineer who, with David Boggs at Xerox PARC in 1973, built the first Ethernet — the link-layer technology that, half a century later, still carries every wired packet from your home router to an 800-gigabit AI training cluster.

## The hook (cold-open)
In 1973, at Xerox PARC, Bob Metcalfe and David Boggs needed to connect a room full of Alto workstations to a laser printer. The first version of what they built ran at 2.94 megabits per second over a single shared coaxial cable. They called it Ethernet. The DIX consortium standardised it in 1980. IEEE ratified it as 802.3 in 1983. Today, four decades on, every wired network on the planet — your home router, a hyperscale data centre, an 800-gigabit AI training fabric — runs Ethernet at Layer 2.

## The work

### Ethernet at Xerox PARC, 1973
The first Ethernet was built by Metcalfe and David Boggs at Xerox PARC to solve a specific local problem — getting Alto workstations to talk to a laser printer. The headline number is 2.94 megabits per second over a shared coaxial cable. We won't unpack the mechanism here; the Ethernet episode walks through framing, addressing, and the carrier-sense multiple-access scheme that made the shared medium work. The biographical fact is that the prototype shipped, inside PARC, in 1973 — and Boggs has his own pioneer episode for the co-inventor side of the story.

### The 1976 CACM paper
In July 1976, Metcalfe and Boggs published "Ethernet: Distributed Packet Switching for Local Computer Networks" in *Communications of the ACM*. That paper is the canonical citation — the moment Ethernet stopped being a PARC project and became a published idea other people could build on.

### The DIX specification and IEEE 802.3
In 1980, Metcalfe co-authored the DIX Ethernet specification — DIX for Digital Equipment, Intel, and Xerox, the three companies that agreed to standardise the technology together. IEEE 802.3 ratified the standard in 1983. From that point on, Ethernet was an industry-wide commodity.

### 3Com, 1979
Metcalfe founded 3Com in 1979 to commercialise Ethernet — adapter cards, controllers, the equipment that made it possible to actually buy and install an Ethernet network. 3Com became one of the defining networking companies of the 1980s and 1990s, and the commercial success is what turned the PARC prototype into the de facto wiring standard for every office building in the world.

## Awards and recognition
Metcalfe received the IEEE Medal of Honor in 1996 and the National Medal of Technology in 2003 for the invention of Ethernet. In 2022 he received the ACM Turing Award — the highest honour in computing — for the same body of work.
